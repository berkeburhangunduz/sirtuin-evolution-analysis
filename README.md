# Evolutionary Analysis of SIRT1 and SIRT5

Evolutionary analysis of SIRT1 and SIRT5 across vertebrate species.

## Overview

This project investigates evolutionary selection patterns in SIRT1 and SIRT5 across vertebrate species using codon-based evolutionary analyses and structural annotation.

### Why SIRT1 and SIRT5?

SIRT1 and SIRT5 were selected as representative members of the sirtuin gene family due to their distinct biological functions and cellular localizations. SIRT1 is a predominantly nuclear sirtuin involved in transcriptional regulation, metabolism, stress responses, and aging-related pathways, whereas SIRT5 is a mitochondrial sirtuin specialized in the regulation of metabolic enzymes through desuccinylation, demalonylation, and deglutarylation activities. Comparing these two functionally divergent sirtuins provides an opportunity to investigate whether different evolutionary pressures have shaped their coding sequences across vertebrate evolution.
## Methods

- Multiple sequence alignment
- Codon alignment
- Datamonkey FEL analysis
- Datamonkey MEME analysis
- AlphaFold structural mapping
- UniProt functional annotation

## Main Findings

### SIRT1

- FEL positive selection sites: 4
- MEME episodic positive selection sites: 10
- Strong overall purifying selection

### SIRT5

- FEL positive selection sites: 3
- MEME episodic positive selection sites: 6
- Positively selected codon 203 identified near Zn²⁺ binding residues (207 and 212)

## Biological Interpretation

Both genes exhibit strong evolutionary conservation, consistent with their essential cellular functions.

SIRT1 showed a greater number of episodically selected codons than SIRT5 (10 MEME sites vs. 6 MEME sites), suggesting a relatively higher level of adaptive evolutionary change.

Despite evidence of positive selection at a limited number of codons, both proteins remained dominated by purifying selection across most sites, indicating preservation of their core functional architecture.

For SIRT5, codon 203 was identified as a positively selected residue located near Zn²⁺ binding residues (207 and 212). This observation suggests that adaptive evolution may have affected regions surrounding functionally important sites while functionally important residues, including the catalytic residue at position 158, remained conserved.
